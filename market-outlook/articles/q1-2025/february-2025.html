Market Overview – February 2025

Tariff Threats and Economic Cracks Shake Markets

February 28, 2025 8 min read Tulga-Ochir Sugar

February was a month of profound contradiction, dominated by an abrupt geopolitical shock that gave way to a creeping realization of a slowing domestic economy. The month began with the administration declaring a trade war on its closest allies, sparking fears of a market "bloodbath." While equities navigated the initial shock to reach new all-time highs, the rally was narrow and fragile. By month-end, a series of weak economic reports exposed cracks in the U.S. economy, causing a sharp pullback and solidifying a defensive posture among investors as policy uncertainty continued to cast a long shadow.

The Tariff Showdown: From Shock to Strategy

The defining event of the month was the administration's announcement of 25% tariffs on Canada and Mexico and an additional 10% on China. Invoked under emergency economic powers, this action immediately overshadowed all other market drivers and set the stage for a week of intense speculation.

The market's initial fear of a "Bloodbath Monday" was tempered by a deeper analysis of the administration's strategy. The unusual structure, which levied higher tariffs on treaty allies than on China, and the delayed Tuesday implementation date, suggested a political maneuver with a built-in "escape clause." The consensus grew that this was a negotiating tactic, especially as Canada and Mexico signaled a surgically precise retaliation strategy aimed at politically sensitive "red states." While the threat continued to cause headline volatility, the probability of full implementation was viewed as low by mid-month, shifting the market's focus to key negotiation deadlines in March.

U.S. Tariff Impact Analysis

By month-end, the administration had begun signaling a more measured approach, with officials suggesting that tariffs could be adjusted based on negotiation outcomes, providing some relief to markets that had been bracing for the worst.

A Twisting Yield Curve and a Cautious Fed

The bond market experienced a turbulent month, characterized by whipsaw price action and a twisting yield curve. While major inflation reports like the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and Producer Price Index (PPI) caused significant intraday volatility, the curve ended each week largely unchanged, masking the underlying tension.

A key dynamic emerged between the short and long ends of the curve. The short end began pricing in a more hawkish Federal Reserve, with almost no rate cuts expected for the next two years. In contrast, the long end rallied on signals from the Treasury of a desire to lower the entire yield curve. This conflict caused the curve to flatten significantly, with the 3-month to 10-year spread narrowing to just 10 basis points, making a second inversion a distinct possibility. Meanwhile, the reverse repo facility (RRP) balance fell to just $58.7 billion, signaling that the Fed would soon be forced to discuss slowing its quantitative tightening program.

Key Market Indicator

The yield curve flattening to just 10 basis points between 3-month and 10-year Treasuries created unusual market dynamics and increased speculation about potential Fed policy shifts.

Fragile Equity Highs and a Stark Divergence

The equity market's performance was a tale of two tapes. The S&P 500 showed remarkable resilience, shaking off the tariff news to post a series of new all-time highs, peaking at 6,212.30 on February 19. However, this rally was built on a weak foundation. Valuations became increasingly stretched, with the forward P/E multiple climbing above 22.6x. Reports indicated that retail investors were the primary buyers of recent dips, a phenomenon dubbed "dip alpha," while institutional investors remained on the sidelines.

Most importantly, a stark divergence emerged between large and small-cap stocks. While the S&P 500 hovered near its peak, the Russell 2000 (IWM) fell back into a 10% correction. This weakness in smaller, more economically-sensitive companies was a clear signal of growing concern about the domestic outlook.

Emerging Cracks in the U.S. Economy

By the end of February, the narrative had shifted decisively from geopolitical threats to domestic economic weakness. A string of disappointing data releases painted a troubling picture:

Weak Retail Sales

The January retail sales report showed a headline decline of 0.9%, with broad-based weakness outside of essentials, pointing to a faltering U.S. consumer.

Slowing Services Sector

The U.S. Services PMI for February dropped to 49.7, falling into contraction territory (<50) for the first time in over a year.

Waning Consumer Confidence: The University of Michigan's consumer sentiment index fell, with 5-year inflation expectations ticking up to a concerning 3.5%.

These reports catalyzed a sharp sell-off into the end of the month, confirming that the combination of policy uncertainty and weakening fundamentals warranted a defensive investment posture: short U.S. beta (especially small caps), long duration (bonds), and negative on the U.S. dollar.

Forward Outlook

February concluded with the market at a crossroads. The economic data points towards a resilient "soft landing," but this constructive backdrop is overshadowed by looming policy risks, particularly on trade. For the rally to be sustained, investors will need to see a continued downtrend in long-term bond yields and, crucially, gain clarity on the new administration's policy agenda. March will be dominated by the Federal Reserve's guidance, the bulk of Q4 earnings reports, and the market's reaction to the first concrete policy actions from Washington.

Key Takeaway

While the market appears to have found its footing after February's volatility, the path forward remains uncertain. Investors should remain vigilant and prepared for potential policy-driven market shifts in the coming months.
