Market Overview – April 2025

The Tariff Crash and a Fragile Truce

April 30, 2025 12 min read Tulga-Ochir Sugar

April 2025 will be remembered as a month of historic, policy-driven chaos. The unilateral imposition of a broad-based tariff triggered a market cataclysm, plunging U.S. equities officially into a bear market and causing a credit crisis. This was followed by a direct and public confrontation between the White House and the Federal Reserve, which further eroded international confidence in U.S. assets. The month ended with a shocking policy U-turn from the administration, sparking a massive relief rally that left the market in a fragile state, entirely dependent on a weakening economy to force the Fed's hand.

The Bloodbath: A Bear Market is Born

The month began with a stunning rout. In direct response to the administration's unilateral 10% across-the-board tariff, U.S. equity markets lost $6.6 trillion in value in a brutal 48-hour sell-off. The damage was swift and severe:

Volatility (VIX) doubled to its highest level in over a year, creating a panic atmosphere across all asset classes. While U.S. Treasuries rallied as the only safe haven, the credit markets signaled extreme distress. Corporate high-yield spreads widened by 22% in a single week, and the 3-month to 10-year Treasury curve plunged deep into inversion (-27 basis points), a classic and reliable recession indicator. The market immediately began pricing in emergency intervention, with futures indicating over 100 basis points of Fed rate cuts within the next three meetings.

Powell vs. Trump: The Fed Draws a Line in the Sand

The crisis escalated into a direct conflict between the nation's monetary and executive branches. In a pivotal speech, Fed Chair Jerome Powell reasserted the central bank's independence, stating there was no "Fed put" to rescue markets from what he described as an orderly reaction to flawed fiscal and trade policy. He called the U.S. debt path "unsustainable" and signaled a patient approach to rate cuts, refusing to be swayed by political pressure.

"There is no 'Fed put' to rescue markets from what is an orderly reaction to flawed fiscal and trade policy."

— Fed Chair Jerome Powell

The White House responded with fury, with President Trump publicly stating Powell's "termination cannot come fast enough." This attack on the Fed's independence sent a shockwave through global markets, leading to the month's most dangerous underlying development.

The Unraveling of the "Safe Haven" Trade

For the first time in the modern era, U.S. assets failed their safe-haven role. Foreign investors, who hold over $8.5 trillion in U.S. debt, found themselves losing on both the U.S. dollar, which entered a structural decline, and U.S. Treasuries, which failed to rally as expected during the equity sell-off.

Historic Shift

For the first time in the modern era, U.S. assets failed their safe-haven role, creating a dangerous feedback loop where foreign selling put upward pressure on U.S. yields.

This forced a repatriation of capital, creating a dangerous feedback loop where foreign selling put upward pressure on U.S. yields, further pressuring the economy and equities. This erosion of the "exorbitant privilege" means the U.S. can no longer count on the world to finance its deficits at low rates. The economic damage was also reflected in the real economy, with the U.S. housing market showing clear signs of distress as active listings surged 27.6% year-over-year and sellers began cutting prices during the normally busy spring season.

A Whiplash U-Turn and a Conditional Rally

The month concluded with a stunning reversal. Facing a market in freefall and a defiant Fed, the administration abruptly softened its stance on firing Powell and its trade disputes. This pivot triggered a massive relief rally, with the S&P 500 soaring 4.63% in a single week.

However, this rally rests on fragile ground. The market's focus has now shifted entirely to the Federal Reserve, which needs "clear permission" from unambiguously negative economic data to begin an easing cycle. The upcoming Q1 GDP report (forecasted by the Atlanta Fed to show a 2.5% contraction) and the April jobs report became the market's new obsession. A deeply negative print in both was paradoxically seen as the "best" outcome for markets, as it would provide the Fed with the justification it needs to act.

Forward Outlook

Despite the sharp rally, the fundamental picture remains bleak. The administration's tariffs and belligerent foreign policy act as a direct tax on the economy, undermining the potential for a productivity boom from technologies like AI. Forward earnings estimates for the S&P 500 continued to fall even as the index's valuation climbed back above a 20x multiple. The consensus investment strategy remains to short rallies, with a fair value for the S&P 500 estimated to be in the 4,250 to 4,680 range, implying significant downside from April's closing levels.
